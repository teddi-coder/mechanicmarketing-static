9 Warning Signs of the Most Common Car Repairs

1 Apr 2025 · 5 min read

Cars break down. It happens. But some problems are way more common than others. Knowing what tends to go wrong can save you money and stress — catch issues early and they won't turn into expensive disasters.

#1 — Worn-Out Brake Pads and Rotors

Brake pads wear out from normal use, but if you ignore them, they grind down to nothing and start eating into your rotors. Rotors are pricey to replace, so catching bad pads early saves significant cash. If your car is struggling to stop or making strange noises, don't ignore it. Get your brakes checked during oil changes — most mechanics will inspect them for free. If you hear squeaking, don't wait. That's the warning the pads are nearly gone.

#2 — Batteries That Die Out of Nowhere

Car batteries usually last 3 to 5 years. Extreme weather, forgetting to turn off the lights, or not driving enough can shorten that. A weak battery often struggles for weeks before giving up entirely — if your car starts slower than usual, that's your first hint. Test your battery every few months, especially before winter and summer. If it's over three years old, get it tested before you're stuck in a car park needing a jump-start.

#3 — Flat or Burst Tyres

Potholes, nails, and low tyre pressure can all lead to flats. Driving on worn-out tyres is risky and can cause blowouts, especially at speed. Check air pressure weekly — underinflated tyres wear out faster and increase fuel consumption. Rotate your tyres every 5,000 to 7,000 km to keep them wearing evenly.

#4 — Check Engine Light That Won't Go Away

The check engine light could mean anything from a loose fuel cap to serious engine trouble. Some drivers ignore it until their car starts running rough or stops entirely. A loose fuel cap is an easy fix — if tightening it doesn't help, get a mechanic to plug in a diagnostic scanner and read the code. Don't ignore it hoping it goes away.

#5 — Overheating Engine

An overheating engine is a serious warning. Causes include low coolant, a leaking radiator, a faulty thermostat, or a failing water pump. If your temperature gauge climbs into the red, pull over safely and turn off the engine. Continuing to drive an overheating car can cause severe internal damage that costs thousands to repair.

#6 — Oil Leaks

Oil leaks are easy to spot — look for puddles under your parked car or a burning oil smell when the engine is warm. Common causes include worn gaskets, loose drain plugs, or damaged seals. Low oil levels cause rapid engine wear, so if you notice a leak, get it looked at promptly. Regular oil checks between services catch leaks before they become critical.
