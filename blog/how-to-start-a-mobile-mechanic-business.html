How to Start a Mobile Mechanic Business in 7 Steps

31 Mar 2025 · 8 min read

If you're great at fixing cars but sick of working for someone else, starting a mobile mechanic business could be your way out. You don't need a workshop or a huge investment to get rolling — but you do need to handle the legal stuff, gear up properly, price your services smartly, and market yourself in a way that gets noticed.

Step 1 — Handle the Legal Stuff Early

Before you touch a customer's car, get your paperwork sorted. Register your business — a sole trader or PTY LTD structure affects tax and liability, so speak with an accountant. Sort any required licences and permits (some states require trade certifications for mobile mechanics — check with your local council). Get public liability insurance and cover for your work vehicle. Open a business bank account and apply for your ABN.

Step 2 — Set Up Your Ute or Van Properly

Your ute or van is your mobile workshop, so it needs to be practical and organised. Fit it with shelving, toolboxes, or drawers so everything has a place. Essential tools include a diagnostic scanner, impact wrench, jack stands, battery tester, and hand tools. Keep common parts like spark plugs, filters, and fuses on hand so you're not running to the parts store mid-job. Add a portable generator or high-capacity battery system for power on-site.

Step 3 — Nail Your Pricing

Research what other mobile mechanics in your area charge. Factor in your time, travel, parts markup, insurance, and vehicle costs. Many mobile mechanics charge a call-out fee plus an hourly rate. Be transparent with customers upfront — clear, honest pricing builds trust and reduces disputes. Don't undercharge to win work early — it sets a price expectation that's hard to raise later.

Step 4 — Get Found Online Fast

Set up a Google Business Profile on day one — it's free and puts you on Google Maps immediately. Ask your first few customers for Google reviews. Build a simple website with your services, service area, and contact details. Post in local Facebook groups and community pages. Car clubs and local forums are also excellent early sources of customers.

Step 5 — Build Referral Networks

Partner with tow truck drivers, roadside assistance providers, and local car dealerships. These are high-value referral sources who regularly encounter stranded or troubled drivers. Offer a referral fee or reciprocal arrangement. Word of mouth spreads fast in the trades — every great job is a potential source of three more.
